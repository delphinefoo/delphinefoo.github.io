This is my first foray into blogging with static website generator Jekyll.

What is Jekyll? It's an open source parsing engine that helps you easily build websites using templates. No, Jekyll doesn't build the templates; you have to do that yourself. This ain't Wordpress, buddy. You can also include 'partials': elements that you design, like headers, footers, or sidebars, that you can reuse in any of your pages.

The magic of mixing and matching using templates and partials is created through Jekyll's use of the Liquid, a Ruby templating language.

Jekyll is particularly useful for text-heavy blogs (like mine will be -- spoiler alert!), since it has the ability to parse files that are named using a particular naming convention, and create new pages on your site directly from those files. What's more, if you don't enjoy writing html, you can write your blog posts in markdown, and Jekyll will still parse them for you. Genius!

If, like me, you're fairly new to programming, all of the above descriptions might sound like a lot of unintelligible jargon. If that's your take on it, a good way to see how Jekyll works might be to poke around some of the example sites that currently use Jekyll, as well as their source code, and learn how the files relate to each other. Also, there's a great article in Smashing Magazine about the why and how-to-start of Jekyll. And, of course, read the Jekyll docs.

Happy blogging!
